abstract: This study analyzes the impact of an in-store stockpiling awareness campaign conducted in Hiroshima Prefecture on disaster preparedness behavior, using point-of-sale (POS) data. As opposed to conventional disaster preparedness education, this campaign aimed to engage a broader segment of the population by leveraging routine shopping environments. A Difference-in-Differences analysis indicated a slight increase in the purchases of cup noodles and instant noodles, while purchases of bottled water were negative. Although in-store campaigns are expected to lower the psychological and practical barriers to disaster preparedness compared to traditional mitigation campaigns, the observed behavioral changes were limited. This finding suggest that further refinement of campaign design is needed.
